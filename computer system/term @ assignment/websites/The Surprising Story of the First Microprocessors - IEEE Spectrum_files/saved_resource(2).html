    A few things missing in this article. First the 4004: Matt Biewer designed the 4004 into a grocery order entry system for MSI data in the summer of 1972. It was in full production by fall and continued for many years. He and I then began Pro-Log around the 4004.. built PROM programmers for the 1702 PROM and all PROMs which followed it and which sold for the next decade. We came out with a single board 4004 based computer in the spring of 1973 which went into machine control, traffic control, elevator control systems. Sold enough units to have National Semiconductor second source 4004 and 4040. We updated to the 4040 (next generation 4004) as well as 8008 and 8080, etc. Not to mention Motorola's 6800, 68000.
    Rockwell also had a chip set in 1971, too complicated to program so we dropped it in favor of 4004.
    This history is a good start, but the more complete story.... including the rise and fall of Motorola in the business.... is even more fascinating.
    In the fall of 72 Matt and I went to Intel's head of marketing and told him we were starting a company around the 4004 microprocessor because microprocessors were going to revolutionize engineering. He said we were nuts because there weren't more than 1000 customer for microprocessors and Intel already knew who they were. Intel was a memory company at the time... with PROMs as its latest memory product.

        First, thanks for your work at Pro-Log. You all did a great job of providing microcomputers to the 'real world'. I designed the F-14 microprocessor, the Jolt series, and the SYM boards and about 300 end user projects. Many times we used Pro-Log products as they fit the end user application. Your story about Intel was certainly true. They were very short-sighted in their marketing. The same year you talked to Intel they hired myself and Manny Lemas as consultants to travel the country and teach engineers how to program. I sat in several Intel marketing meetings where they {desperately} discussed how to sell their microprocessors as Noyce wanted to drop the product. For two years Intel sent us around the country teaching engineers a one week 4004, 8008, and PL/M programming class (Gary Kildahl taught the PL/M) to Intel 'memory' customers HOPING they would adopt the 'programming your design' concept over fixed logic design. In those two year we probably taught over 1000 engineers how to program. The most dramatic conversion was the National Cash Register company whose VP of Engineering attended our class and 'saw the light' on how programming your design saved time and money. He went back and began the conversion of all of their products to microprocessors-based.

                                        Neal.Laurance@ieee.org Ray Holt • 4 years ago

                                        Ray,

                                        You are probably conflating two or more meetings. In the 1970’s, the auto industry was under a court injunction that absolutely forbade any joint development with competitors. There is no way a Ford person and a GM person would be in the same room at such a meeting. I know that we (i.e Ford) had such a meeting with Fairchild in the fall of 1971; GM probably did as well.

                                        That fall, we visited TI, Fairchild, AMD and Intel, soliciting interest in working with the auto industry. Our impression was that the industry was on the verge of explosive growth, and had inadequate resources to address it. The auto industry applications looked to be resource intensive and the industry wanted pick more low hanging fruit. Everyone was polite and enthusiastic, of course, but there was little follow through.

                                        There was one person at Fairchild, whose name I have forgotten, who was actively promoting work with the auto companies. He authored several pieces on the subject. My impression is that he was ignored at Fairchild.

                                        As for lack of vision on the part of the auto industry, at that time our vision was well tempered with skepticism. We already had seen the 4004, and had the design specs for the 8008. I had a working simulator of the 8008 running on a time sharing system, and it seemed to be inadequate for our initial task of controlling spark and exhaust gas flow. At 3000 rpm, in a V 8 engine, one has 5 milliseconds between spark plug firings. Our target was to recalculate the spark timing for each firing. That entailed a table lookup and interpolation to 0.1 % accuracy. The 8008 fell far short of that.

                                        Fuel control was something for the future. We were still using carburetors, and fuel injection (for SI engines) was just in its infancy.

                                        As it turned out, our initial development was supported by RCA, by Essex Wire (later part of UTC) and by Toshiba, all large corporations with more resources. Silicon Valley didn’t get involved until the last ha lf of the 1970’s.

                                        Neal Laurance

                                                      Jack Carsten • 5 years ago

                                                      My name is Jack Carsten, and I was the GM of TI's MOS Division from 1971-1974 and the VP of Marketing of Intel from 1975-1979. As such, all of the "inventors" of the microprocessor at both TI and Intel were in my organizations, and we vigorously debated the definition and origins of the chips mentioned. It is true that Vic Poor at CTC stimulated both TI and Intel to build a general purpose 8 bit CPU to replace TTL MSI (built by TI) being used in their desktop calculators. However, the TI chip never completely worked, and Poor rejected the Intel 8008 design as too slow and too expensive, and continued to use TTL/MSI in production.

                                                      With respect to the definition of the microprocessor by author Shirriff, I believe he is fogging the issues regarding many multichip CPUs built of MOS as far back as 1963 (The Apollo guidance computer and the TI "Blackbird" SCL used in the spook plane in 1964). These used early FSC and TI chips announced in 1962-64. The first single chip CPUs were the Intel 4004 and the TI TMS0100, both introduced in 1971. The distinction which is significant is that the TMS0100 (later TMS 1000) was a one chip calculator that was VENDOR programmed only, while the Intel 4004 was designed to be USER programmed, a concept that TI rejected at the time. So if we define a microprocessor as a single chip CPU that is user programmed, then Intel was first, as is generally recognized.

                                                      All of this information is well documented by the Computer History Museum and by Wikichips (which see).

                                                            Jim Schaffner • 5 years ago

                                                            GREAT article. Thanks, Ken! Additionally, I find the comments posted here as interesting as the article. I too am a digital design guy who worked thru all of that tumult of the 1970's, Couple of points... A lot of companies tried to make their own "microprocessor." And, again,success depended upon the definition used, I worked for General Automation Inc (GA) in Anaheim - just down the street from Autonetics. The GA president and major founder, Larry Goshorn, had led the design of a 2-chip, bipolar (SOS, mind you!) "microprocessor". The chips were: ALU/Reg File & Control ROM. Each chip was a behemoth 64-pin ceramic DIP, and the cost was off the charts. But Larry thought he knew better than anyone and wouldn't even consider allowing any engineer at GA to design any board product using anything but. The then popular Intel 8080 et al. were burgeoning, but all GA board design "shall use only the GA Microprocessor, and that's final", he decreed. Well, a couple a years later, GA was just another dead carcass on the long digital hi-way. Hey, Ken Olsen torqued DEC similarly. But Intel succeeded where almost All others failed because of their recognition that the new center of the universe was software, not hardware anymore. At the time, as i recall, at least 25 silicon makers had their own real, single-chip micorP. But Intel and Moto, a little later, supported the 8080 via a software development system: a big blue rack with 3 units: a small computer running a 8080 linker/assembler+dual 5.25" floppy discs, a WCS - writable control store memory unit - with the requisite ICE clip and a power supply at the rack bottom. Using this tool one could actually create a real uP based product. Little did we Hardware Engineers know that we were doomed from that day on.

                                                            Regards,
                                                            Yogi (Jim) Schaffner

                                                                      David Warman • 5 years ago

                                                                      I was designing logic stuff through that period, for a small company. The market was truly chaotic, and I recall there were other chips becoming available, including one from GM or NMI (forget exactly) that came from NASA origins. But all were too expensive for my small company. The 4004, considered for my first keboard entry system, cost as much for the dev system as several years of my salary, so I did it myself using bipolar. Used a reference 4004 box to program my 27C02 program store. Next effort was a four board bipolar design using the new 7483(?), same economics applied this time re the 8008, and a coproc for it to handle all the then extant different encoding techniques for the new floppy drives. I learned an incredible amount designing those beasties; the first one saved the company and the second design was used in internal production for several years after I left the company.

                                                                      Point here: there was a lot more going on then. Apart from my work, we also factored Wyse terminals, several glass TTYs for which I would hack their control logic for custom requests, Tek 4010 graphics terminals, for which I also designed custom interfaces, and discovered the utility of PALs as gp logic blocks, and this just from a small then unknown company's perspective. Microprocs were definitely in the air just waiting for economics and technology to catch up. Seemed more useful as microcontrollers initially, indeed there are probably still more micrcontroller uses today (still pay my salary for example).

                                                                                                    For those interested I have finally published my story of designing the world's first microprocessor in any working application. As mentioned all over the net, it was for the F-14 Tomcat fighter jet and used the exact technology as the 4004 but two years earlier. Six chip types and no support TTL, unlike the 4000 - series, mil spec, in-flight diagnostics, with no in-flight failures for 30 years.
